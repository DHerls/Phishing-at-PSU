What is phishing?

Phishing is the act of creating a fake website, email, or other ruse in order to trick you into disclosing your password or other sensitive information to criminals.

Why is this important?

If this were a real phishing site, you might never even know that your password had been compromised. Once someone has your password, they can do a lot of damage before you realize anything is wrong. Imagine waking up one day and finding someone had dropped all of your classes! But it's not just your Penn State account you have to worry about. There are phishing scams going on for every major website you can think of. Plus, over 90% of internet users use one password, or a variation of one password, for all of their accounts, so if a hacker gets one of your passwords, he basically has all of your passwords.

How can you protect yourself from phishing?

There will always be those online who are looking to steal your password, so the best thing you can do is learn how to protect yourself so you don't accidentally fall for a real scam down the line.

Check the URL

If you look in the address bar of your browser, you'll see that this website is psuits.org, not psu.edu. Likewise you can see the email you received was sent from @psuits.org, not @psu.edu. Whenever you receive an email asking for personal information or for you to log into an account, always check who it's really from and make sure it's who you were expecting.

Links in emails

One of the best ways to protect yourself from phishing attacks is not clicking any links in emails, especially if you don't recognize the URL. Someone could make a link that looks like www.google.com actually redirect to something else. Go ahead and click that link and you'll see what I mean. This is what I did in the email you received. Even though it looked like a real Penn State website, it redirected you here. If you do get an email with a link in it, you should always copy and paste it into your browser instead of just clicking. That way you know exactly where you're going.

2-Factor Authentication

2-Factor Authentication means you need two pieces of information to log into your account, not just your password. The most common method of doing this is having a code sent to your cell phone that you have to type in after your password. That way even if someone gets your password, they won't be able to access your account. Most popular websites, and Penn State as well, offer 2-Factor authentication. You can find how to turn on 2-Factor Authentication for your Penn State account here. Please be aware though that 2FA is not a magic shield. If someone is targeting you specifically, which is called spear-phishing, then 2-Factor Authentication will only slow the attacker down slightly, and if they are prepared, it won't slow them down at all.

Password Managers

In the event that your password is compromised, the best thing you can do is minimize impact. This means making sure you don't use the same password for any other websites. Ideally you would have a random password for every website, but we can't remember all those passwords. The best way to solve that is with a password manager. A good password manager will keep all your passwords encrypted and will let you make a random one for every website you visit. That way you only need to remember the password to get into your vault and the password manager does the rest. There are numerous options such as LastPass, which is what I use and recommend, 1Password, KeePass, and several others.

How can Penn State protect its students and faculty from identity theft and phishing?

Educate People on the Dangers of Phishing

The best way to ensure that people avoid phishing is to educate them about the dangers that exist online and how realistic they can appear. When Penn State is informing new students about Access Accounts and how they can be used, a page could be added about the dangers of online phishing. It could include information on how to avoid compromising your Penn State account and could have similar information as what appears on this page.

Upgrade to an Extended Validation SSL Certificate

An SSL certificate is a digital signature that gives websites that little green lock icon and in the top left corner of your address bar. When you see that icon, the data being sent back and forth to the website is encrypted, which means no one else can read it. However, that is only a standard SSL certificate, and anyone can get one of those. If you look in the corner of your address bar right now, you'll see that THIS website even has an SSL certificate. An Extended Validation SSL Certificate goes one step further and allows users to see just who is behind a website. If you go to Twitter, you can see that instead of just a lock icon, it says "Twitter Inc." No matter how good a phishing site looks, and how similar it actually looks to Twitter, it will never have "Twitter Inc." in the corner. I propose that Penn State upgrade to an Extended Validation SSL so everyone will know which websites are actually run by Penn State.

More Restrictions on the Penn State Directory

All of the information that I used to create the fake emails, namely full names combined with email addresses, came from the Penn State directory. If the directory itself was entirely behind WebAccess, I wouldn't have been able to scrape all the information that I did as easily. At the very least, some sort of brute-force protection would dramatically decrease the ability of attackers to gather target information. In its current configuration, I was able to download 110,786 records in around an hour.

The Client-Side Code

To view the page source: go back to the fake login page, webaccess.psuits.org, right click on the page, then click "View Source". If you look, you will find a line containing function sendUsername(). That is the JavaScript function that is called whenever someone hits the login button. If you look slightly lower in the section called data, you will see that I only grab document.f.username. If I wanted your password, I would have to grab document.f.password as well. You can also see that the function returns false, which means the login form itself does not submit. All this combined means your password never leaves your computer.
